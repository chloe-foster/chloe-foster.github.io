Website Accessibility

Even with legal protections such as the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 and the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), keeping society accessible is a continuous struggle. Doubly so on the internet, a place where accessibility regulations have only begun to reach. In hindsight, not accounting for this initially was a forgiveable mistake - at the time, I'm pretty sure nobody predicted just how much the internet would revolutionize nearly every corner of modern life. (Sapega 2020) But at this point, the internet has long since stopped being the hip new thing. There's not really any space left for excuses. Even the law is coming around on the subject of internet accessibility - in 1996, the Department of Justice asserted that the ADA also extended to the internet. (Sapega 2020)

Make the internet more accessible. At least, to the extent possible from your corner of the internet. How do you start?

Some part of your userbase will be comprised of disabled people, so someone will always appreciate the 10 or so extra minutes you put into making a page accessible to satisfy legal requirements. Work put into website accessibility isn't just going to be appreciated by those who need it, thanks to the curb-cut effect - changes made in the name of accessibility for one or a few groups make things better for everyone. (Blackwell 2017)

    Straightforward changes to make:

    Note: this is not an exhaustive list.
  • People with cognitive disabilities, such as myself, need good color contrast, more easily parseable text, and relatively uncluttered web pages. (Burgstahler 2014) (I personally heavily rely on an open-source implementation of Bionic Reading for walls of text.) This also helps neurotypical people, who still benefit to a lesser extent from clearly-presented content.
  • People who are blind need compatibility with screen reading software via use of semantical HTML tags (Burgstahler 2014); ensuring this also ensures that automated search engine crawlers can more easily parse the page and improve search ranking, too. (Mozilla 2023)
  • People who are deaf need captions to understand anything involving audio. (Burgstahler 2014) Captions, however, help virtually everyone, from people with audio processing difficulties to people who simply don't have a suitable audio output on hand. (Gernsbacher 2015)
  • People with motor disabilities may not be able to use a mouse; websites must be fully and easily navigable using a keyboard alone. (Burgstahler 2014) Properly implemented keyboard navigation, broadly speaking, is also faster than navigating via mouse, helping those who need to use your website quickly or frequently.
